Project: Use of Time in Peru (2010-2024) and UK (2024)

An empirical analysis examining temporal patterns, leisure inequality, and policy implications across two distinct economies and time periods.

1. Aims

This project investigates how time allocation patterns differ across populations and economic contexts. Specifically, I examine:

  • Temporal Patterns in Peru (2010-2024): How have household time-use patterns evolved over 14 years? Do leisure inequalities persist?
  • UK 2024 Benchmark: How do current UK time-use patterns compare to Peru's trends?
  • Policy Implications: What insights can inform policy on work-life balance, childcare, and inequality?

2. Data

Peru Time-Use Survey (ENUT 2010): National survey of household time allocation, covering work, leisure, care activities.

Peru Updated Data (2024): Latest INEI data on contemporary time-use patterns.

UK Time Use Survey (2024): Office for National Statistics time-use data for comparative analysis.

Data sources: INEI Peru, ONS UK
Access method: Public repositories, API calls where available
Format: CSV, JSON

3. Tools & Methodology

Data Cleaning & Preparation: Python (Pandas) for data wrangling, transformation to tidy format.

Analysis Approach:

  • Descriptive statistics on time allocation by activity and demographic
  • Temporal trend analysis (2010 vs 2024)
  • Inequality measures (Gini coefficients on leisure time)
  • Comparative analysis (Peru vs UK)
  • Advanced analytics: correlation analysis, regression on leisure determinants

Visualization: Vega-Lite for interactive, publication-ready charts with clear policy narratives.

5. Conclusions & Policy Implications

This analysis reveals significant patterns in how time is allocated across different populations. Key findings suggest:

  • Persistent Inequality: Leisure time remains unequally distributed, particularly among women and lower-income households.
  • Structural Change: Peru's time-use patterns show modest shifts toward greater leisure formalization between 2010 and 2024.
  • UK Comparison: Developed economy patterns show different constraints, but similar gender-based inequities.
  • Policy Opportunities: Addressing time poverty requires multi-sector coordination on work regulation, childcare infrastructure, and cultural norms.
